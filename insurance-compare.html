Why "primary vs. secondary medical" matters specifically for you

This is the single biggest difference between IMG and Generali, and it's worth 60 seconds of attention because it's a confusing topic that's poorly explained in most marketing.

You're 71 and on Medicare. Medicare provides no coverage outside the US — none. So when people compare "primary vs. secondary" travel insurance, the practical question for someone on Medicare is: how much paperwork is between you and getting paid?

With IMG (primary): You're hospitalized in Johannesburg. IMG calls the hospital, agrees to direct billing, the hospital treats you, IMG pays the hospital. You sign one form. Done.

With Generali (secondary): You're hospitalized in Johannesburg. You pay the hospital out-of-pocket (or your travel companion does). When you get home, you file a claim with Medicare. Medicare denies it (because it's not US-based). You then file with Generali, attaching the Medicare denial letter and itemized hospital bills, and wait 30-60 days for Generali to reimburse you. (Source: Squaremouth Generali review and Generali's own claims process documentation)

Both end with the same financial outcome eventually. But "eventually" with a foreign hospital bill in the tens of thousands of dollars on your credit card and a 60-day reimbursement wait is meaningfully worse than "directly paid by IMG." This is the structural difference that shows up only when you're actually using the policy.

Why Generali's pre-existing waiver structure is a real problem for your trip

This is more subtle but more important than it sounds.

The pre-existing condition waiver protects you if any chronic condition you have flares up before/during the trip and causes you to need to cancel or get medical care. For someone 71, this is the single most valuable feature of any travel insurance policy. Without it, the most likely cause of a claim — your existing health stuff — is the one thing not covered.

IMG SE waiver rule: "Within 20 days of your initial trip deposit." Your initial deposit is verifiably April 17, 2026 (Mmilo PayFast receipt). Counting forward, deadline is Wednesday May 7. Buy the policy by May 7 and the waiver is locked. Clean. Documented. Anchored to a single date you can verify.

Generali Premium waiver rule: "Before or within 24 hours of FINAL trip payment, AND all prepaid nonrefundable costs must be insured." For your trip, "final trip payment" is fuzzy — Mmilo balance is due May 21, UA flights are now booked, hotels haven't been booked yet. There's no clean "final payment" event. You'd have to either (a) buy the policy in May before booking hotels — but then later hotel bookings might void the waiver if they're considered non-refundable additions, or (b) buy it after your last booking, which could be late June. This is genuinely confusing and creates real risk.

The IMG approach was specifically designed for trips like yours where deposits and final payments are spread over months. Generali's approach was designed for simpler "book it, pay it, insure it" cruise-style trips.
